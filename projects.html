One of the hardest problems I worked on was implementing a standardized barcode and warehouse layout system across three warehouses during my internship at Interlink. The core issue was that each warehouse — and even each floor manager — had their own way of organizing space and counting locations. One person would label locations left to right, another right to left, and none of it was consistent. The WMS we were rolling out needed a standardized structure to work properly, but the physical reality of the warehouses didn’t match that at all. I had to design a standardized location and barcode protocol that could work across all three warehouses and still be practical for how people actually worked on the floor. That meant deciding how locations should be numbered, where barcodes should be placed, and how to prevent people from scanning the wrong thing. I also had to walk a barcode supplier through the warehouses and explain exactly what we needed — where barcodes should go, how high they could be placed so scanners could still reach them, and how they needed to be protected physically. For floor barcodes, we realized they needed metal housings so forklifts wouldn’t damage them, but the metal couldn’t interfere with forklift movement or damage the equipment. There were a lot of real-world edge cases. For example, wheels could get caught between shelving and barcode housings, which would dirty the barcodes and make them unreadable. I came up with a temporary tape solution so we could keep barcodes clean and readable until a permanent fix was implemented. The hardest part wasn’t technical — it was coordinating people, physical constraints, and system requirements into one coherent solution that actually worked in the real world. It taught me how messy real operations are and how important it is to design systems around human behavior and physical reality, not just software requirements.
